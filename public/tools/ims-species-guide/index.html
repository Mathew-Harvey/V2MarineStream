What's This Guide For?

The Job

You're cleaning hulls. You need to spot the bad stuff before you scrape it off. This guide shows you what to look for and what to report.

Why It Matters

Marine pests hitch rides on hulls. Some nasty ones have already turned up in WA - like carpet sea squirt at HMAS Stirling. Divers are our first line of defence.

Invasive marine species hitch rides on ship hulls and in sea chests. Sri Lanka has already recorded new invaders such as the charru mussel (Mytella strigata) near Anawilundawa, and the Asian green mussel is widespread. Divers and inspectors are the first line of defence under the IMO Biofouling Guidelines.

⚠️ Suspect a Noxious Species?

  • STOP cleaning — cease work until DPIRD advises
  • Take photos - close-up + wide shot, use your hand for scale
  • Note where on the hull (bow distance, depth, port/starboard)
  • DON'T scrape it off — leave specimen intact
  • Report to DPIRD FishWatch 1800 815 507 within 24 hours

⚠️ Suspect an Invasive Marine Species?

  • STOP cleaning — pause in-water work until MEPA advises
  • Take photos - close-up + wide shot, use your hand for scale
  • Note where on the hull (sea chest, niche area, depth, port/starboard) and the vessel's last ports
  • DON'T scrape it off — keep the specimen intact for identification
  • Report to the Marine Environment Protection Authority (MEPA) — Tel +94 11 2554373 / +94 11 2554006, email info@mepa.gov.lk (mepa.gov.lk)

Part 1: Invasive Hull Foulers — The Bad Stuff

These are the pests that stick to hulls and need reporting. If you see any of these, follow your reporting protocol.

Carpet Sea Squirt
Didemnum vexillum
NOXIOUS PROHIBITED CONTROLLED DECLARED PROHIBITED DECLARED CLASS A/B
Sea Squirt • WA Noxious Listed Sea Squirt

Nicknamed "sea vomit" - and it looks like it. Creamy-tan carpet that grows over everything. Already found at HMAS Stirling and Henderson.

Nicknamed "sea vomit" - and it looks like it. Creamy-tan carpet that grows over everything. A serious hull and niche-area fouler — report any sightings.

Nicknamed "sea vomit" - and it looks like it. Creamy-tan carpet that grows over everything. HIGH priority pest - report any sightings in NSW waters immediately.

How to Spot It

  • Creamy/tan/orange colour - like old wax
  • Veiny look - like varicose veins
  • Feels leathery, NOT slimy
  • Can drip down like melted candle wax
  • Tiny holes (siphons) all over surface
Don't confuse with: Native colonial sea squirts - those are thinner, more gelatinous, and don't have the veiny/waxy texture.
Asian Green Mussel
Perna viridis
NOXIOUS PROHIBITED CONTROLLED DECLARED PROHIBITED DECLARED CLASS A/B
Mussel • WA Noxious Listed Mussel • NSW Prohibited Mussel • VIC Priority Pest Mussel • TAS Declared Mussel • QLD Prohibited Mussel • SA Declared Mussel • NT Priority

Big mussel with bright green shell. Common on ships coming from Asia. Check sea chests carefully - they love them.

How to Spot It

  • Young ones = bright emerald green
  • Older ones = brownish-green
  • Big - 80-165mm (hand-sized)
  • Inside is blue-green when open
  • Beak points down
Don't confuse with: NZ Green-lipped Mussel - NZ ones are MUCH bigger (240mm vs 165mm), have a distinct green "lip" on shell edge, and are dark brown-green not bright emerald. Blue mussel (native) - those are blue-black, not green.
Black-striped False Mussel
Mytilopsis sallei
NOXIOUS PROHIBITED CONTROLLED DECLARED PROHIBITED DECLARED CLASS A/B
Mussel • WA Noxious Listed Mussel • NSW Prohibited Mussel • VIC Priority Pest Mussel • TAS Declared Mussel • QLD Prohibited Mussel • SA Declared Mussel • NT Priority

Small stripy mussel that forms thick clusters. Loves brackish water so check sea chests and anywhere with mixed water.

How to Spot It

  • Small - only up to 25mm (thumbnail-sized)
  • Dark stripes on shell
  • Packs together in thick layers
  • Often found inside sea chests
Charru Mussel
Mytella strigata
HIGH RISK
Mussel

A small dark mussel that has recently invaded Sri Lanka — first found in 2023 in shrimp farms near the Anawilundawa Ramsar wetland on the north-west coast. Forms dense clusters that foul aquaculture gear, clog nets and intakes, and smother native bivalves. Tolerates brackish water, so check sea chests, internal seawater systems and low-salinity niche areas.

How to Spot It

  • Small — usually 20-40mm long
  • Dark olive-brown to blackish, sometimes with faint rays
  • Glossy, smooth shell with a pointed beak
  • Packs into thick clumps on hard surfaces and on each other
  • Thrives in estuaries, lagoons and brackish water
Don't confuse with: Young Asian green mussels (those are green-tinged and grow much larger) and the black-striped false mussel Mytilopsis sallei (which has internal shelf-like "teeth" inside the shell hinge). If unsure, keep the specimen and report it.

NZ Green-lipped Mussel
Perna canaliculus
WATCH WATCH CONTROLLED DECLARED WATCH DECLARED WATCH
Mussel • Watch Species Mussel • Watch Species Mussel • VIC Priority Pest Mussel • TAS Declared Mussel • Watch Species Mussel • SA Declared Mussel • Watch Species

Big mussel from NZ. Look for vessels that have been to New Zealand recently. NOT in Australia.

Don't confuse with: Asian Green Mussel - Asian ones are smaller (max 165mm), bright emerald green when young, and lack the distinctive green "lip" on shell edge.
Large Non-Native Oysters
Magallana spp.
WATCH
Oyster • Hard to ID in-situ

Large oysters are virtually impossible to identify to species in the water. Includes Pacific (M. gigas), Black Scar (M. bilineata) and Suminoe (M. ariakensis) oysters. Photograph any large oysters for lab ID.

How to Spot It

  • Messy irregular shell - no neat shape
  • Can get huge - up to 400mm
  • Rough frilly edges - sharp!
  • Glued flat to the surface
Black Scar Oyster
Magallana bilineata
PRESENT ESTABLISHED
Oyster • Present in NT Oyster • Established in North QLD

Large tropical oyster now established on the north Queensland coast from Townsville to Cape Bedford. First detected in 2019 (Cairns, Port Douglas, Cooktown). Distinctive dark adductor muscle scar on both valves. Report any new sightings outside known range.

Large tropical oyster present in one marina in the NT. Also established on the north QLD coast. Distinctive dark adductor muscle scar on both valves. Report any sightings.

How to Spot It

  • Large — up to 212mm shell length
  • Dark adductor scar on BOTH valves
  • Pale yellow or purplish shell
  • Thin, flaky shell layers
  • Green iridescent hinge (when fresh)
  • Found in estuaries on rocks/pilings
Don't confuse with: Sydney Rock Oyster or Pacific Oyster — Black Scar has distinctive dark muscle scar visible on both shell valves. No chomata (shell margin pits).

Mussel

Your everyday Aussie mussel. Blue-black shell in clumps. You'll scrape heaps of these.

  • Pointy triangle shape
  • Clumps together with threads

Barnacle

  • Diamond-shaped opening at top
  • Glued directly to surface
  • Can be packed solid

Barnacle

Serpulid Tube Worms

Tube Worm

White calcareous tubes with small feathery fans. Tiny spiral ones (spirorbids) and bigger straight ones.

  • Larger serpulids are straight/curved
  • Small feathery fan - NOT the big spiral fan of European fan worm

Demospongiae
Sponge

Soft spongy sheets in various colours. Orange, yellow, grey. Has tiny holes (oscules) all over. Note: In the photo, the pink encrusting growth is the sponge — other organisms in the image (e.g. smooth rubbery sheets) may be colonial ascidians, which can look similar at first glance.

How to Spot It

  • Sheet or cushion growth on hard surfaces
  • Various colours — often orange, pink, yellow, grey
  • Soft and spongy to touch (not rubbery like ascidians)
  • Tiny pores (oscules) visible — key difference from ascidians
  • Crumbly texture when broken (ascidians are more rubbery)
Don't confuse with: Colonial ascidians (sea squirts) — ascidians are smooth and rubbery, squirt water when squeezed, and lack the tiny pores. Sponges are softer, more crumbly, and have visible pore openings.
Native Colonial Sea Squirts
Didemnidae (native)
Sea Squirt

Thin jelly-like mats. Similar to the invasive Didemnum but thinner and more gelatinous. Be careful with ID.

How to Spot It

  • Thin gelatinous mats
  • Various colours
  • Thinner than invasive carpet sea squirt
  • No veiny/waxy texture
Could be confused with: Carpet sea squirt (invasive) - if in doubt, report it. The invasive one is thicker, veiny, and feels leathery not slimy.
Solitary Sea Squirts (Native)
Cnemidocarpa spp.
Sea Squirt

Single blob-shaped animals, orange or white. Two holes on top (siphons).

How to Spot It

  • Orange/white colour
  • Single blobs, not colonies
  • Two openings on top
  • Leathery skin
Sea Lettuce
Ulva australis
Algae

Bright green thin sheets. Looks like salad leaves. Common in nutrient-rich ports.

How to Spot It

  • Bright green
  • Thin see-through sheets
  • Ruffled wavy edges
  • Soft like lettuce

How to Spot It

  • Branching threads
  • Tangled mats

  • Fuzzy appearance

Hairy Mussel
Trichomya hirsuta
Mussel

Native mussel with fuzzy hairy shell. Brown/black with distinctive bristly covering.

How to Spot It

  • Hairy/fuzzy shell covering
  • Brown/black colour
  • Dense clumps
  • Up to 60mm
Sydney Rock Oyster
Saccostrea glomerata
Native NSW Oyster

Native NSW oyster. Rounder and smaller than the invasive Pacific Oyster. Less frilly edges.

How to Spot It

  • Rounder and smaller than Pacific Oyster
  • Usually less than 80mm
  • Less frilly edges
  • Native to NSW waters
Compare with: Pacific Oyster (invasive) - larger, more irregular shape, very frilly sharp edges.
